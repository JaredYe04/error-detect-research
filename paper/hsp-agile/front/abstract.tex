\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}

Ordered guard specifications say which scenario must fire first when several
guards hold.
Large language models rarely obey that rule: they look right on everyday
inputs and fail on the thin overlaps that formal contracts are meant to pin
down.
Unit tests almost never sample those regions, so test-based repair leaves the
defects in place.

Three insights drive prevention.
First, the check should depend on \emph{semantic regions} (first-match
partitions), not on the surface syntax of any one notation---a shared
intermediate form realises that idea.
Second, repair needs a \emph{typed failure record} that names the violated
scenario and witness, not raw pass/fail bits.
Third, release must be \emph{conjunctive}: every witness matches \emph{and} no
high-severity structural pattern remains.
The \textbf{Specification-guided Defect Prevention Framework} (\textsc{SgDP}),
instantiated as \textbf{HSP-Agile} for SOFL/FSF, is the vehicle for those
three ideas.

On a 120-task overlap-intensive benchmark, HSP-Agile reaches 90.4\% mean
formal conformance (+6.2\,pp over one-shot LLM).
Against test-feedback (B2), the aggregate gap is modest and \emph{not
significant} after Holm--Bonferroni correction ($p{=}0.811$); M wins 6/120
paired tasks ($\approx$5\%), with the margin concentrating where guard regions
overlap.
Structured Semantic Feedback IR alone accounts for +7.7\,pp over test-only
prompts (E6); conjunctive acceptance trades a lower strict-success rate
(25.0\%) for fewer escaped defects.
Extended self-refinement baselines (B3--B5, evaluated) and uniform random (E10)
and external SOFL (E11) benchmarks bound external validity.
The same loop transfers to Mini-Z and Mini-StateMachine with adapter-level
changes.
Tasks, logs, and scripts are released.

\paragraph{Keywords.}
Specification-guided defect prevention; formal specification; LLM synthesis; SOFL;
counterexample-guided repair; SMT verification; mutation testing.
