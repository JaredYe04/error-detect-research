\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}

Large language models generate code that often looks plausible yet violates
ordered guard semantics in formal specifications.
We call these failures \emph{specification-conformance defects}: they hide in
low-measure boundary regions that ordinary unit tests rarely sample, so
test-based repair loops systematically miss them.

We present the \textbf{Specification-guided Defect Prevention Framework}
(\textsc{SgDP}) and its SOFL/FSF instantiation \textbf{HSP-Agile}.
The key idea is to treat the specification itself as the evidence source:
SMT solving builds witnesses that activate each scenario under first-match
precedence, and a typed Semantic Feedback IR turns each failure into a
scenario-aware repair prompt.
A conjunctive acceptance gate then releases a candidate only when every
witness matches the oracle and no high-severity structural pattern remains.

On a 120-task precedence-sensitive benchmark, HSP-Agile reaches 90.4\% mean
strict formal conformance (+6.2\,pp over one-shot LLM, +2.4\,pp over
test-feedback).
Mechanism studies show that the gain grows with guard overlap, that SMT
witnesses keep boundary coverage where random sampling fails, and that the
Semantic Feedback IR alone contributes +7.7\,pp over test-only repair prompts
(E6).
Adapter-level experiments on Mini-Z and Mini-StateMachine confirm the same
prevention loop transfers across notations.
All tasks, logs, and scripts are released for reproduction.

\paragraph{Keywords.}
Specification-guided defect prevention; formal specification; LLM synthesis; SOFL;
counterexample-guided repair; SMT verification; mutation testing.
