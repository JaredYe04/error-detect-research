\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}

LLM-generated implementations systematically fail to respect the ordered guard semantics of
formal specifications, producing a class of defects we call \emph{specification-conformance
defects}.  These defects are missed by existing test-based repair loops because they
manifest only in the low-measure boundary regions that formal specifications are
specifically designed to characterise.

We present the \textbf{Specification-guided Defect Prevention Framework} (\textsc{SgDP}),
a general four-component architecture that grounds defect prevention in specification
semantics at every stage: a \emph{Specification Oracle} derived directly from the formal
spec, a \emph{Constraint-guided Witness Generator} using SMT solving to navigate to
semantically critical boundary regions, a \emph{Scenario-aware Semantic Feedback IR}
structuring counterexample information at the specification level, and a conjunctive
\emph{Acceptance Predicate} gating release on both formal conformance and
specification-derived quality criteria.
\textbf{HSP-Agile} instantiates \textsc{SgDP} over the SOFL Functional Specification Format
(FSF).

We establish three formal results: Scenario-aware Witness Soundness (Theorem~1),
a Finite-Sample FAR Bound (Proposition~2), and an empirically supported hypothesis of
improvement under structured feedback (Hypothesis~3).
We evaluate on a benchmark of 120 precedence-sensitive hard tasks (plus 40 real-derived
tasks from HumanEval/MBPP converted to FSF-style ordered scenarios) across nine execution
modes (including Self-Refine and Self-Debug extended baselines), with a five-dimension
evaluation protocol spanning overall effectiveness (E1), full prevention evaluation (E2),
specification complexity analysis (E3), boundary density analysis (E4), repair dynamics
(E5), feedback variant comparison (E6), pattern guard precision/recall (E7), and
generalisation across 18-task Mini-Z and Mini-StateMachine notations (E8).

HSP-Agile achieves 90.4\% mean strict formal conformance (+6.2~pp over one-shot LLM,
+2.4~pp over test-feedback).  Mechanism analysis confirms that the advantage grows with
specification complexity, that SMT witnesses maintain boundary coverage where random
sampling fails, and that the Semantic Feedback IR produces faster repair convergence than
test-only feedback.  Generalisation experiments demonstrate consistent improvement across
three specification notations with only adapter-level changes.

\paragraph{Keywords.}
Specification-guided defect prevention; formal specification; LLM synthesis; SOFL;
counterexample-guided repair; SMT verification; mutation testing.
