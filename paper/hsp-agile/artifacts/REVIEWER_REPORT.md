---

## 1. Overall Recommendation

**Borderline (lean Weak Reject)**

The three-insight framing (SpecIR regions, Semantic Feedback IR, conjunctive acceptance) is clear and the formalization is unusually careful for SE+LLM work, but the headline comparative story does not survive skeptical scrutiny: the primary benchmark is author-built and overlap-filtered in the method’s favor, M vs test-feedback B2 is **not significant** after Holm correction (p=0.811, δ≈0), and supplementary E10/E11/E12 runs often favor B2 or fail to stabilize the ranking. This reads as a strong technical report/thesis chapter, not yet a conference-ready empirical win over practical baselines.

---

## 2. Summary

The paper proposes **SgDP**, instantiated as **HSP-Agile** for SOFL/FSF ordered-guard specifications. LLM-generated code frequently violates first-match scenario precedence on thin guard-overlap regions that random unit tests rarely hit. The framework lowers specifications to **SpecIR**, generates **Z3 witnesses** per strict first-match region, repairs via a typed **Semantic Feedback IR**, and releases only under a **conjunctive** gate (full witness conformance ∧ no high-severity pattern hits). On a 120-task synthetic hard benchmark (single repeat, Qwen2.5-27B-Instruct), mode **M** reaches **90.4%** mean formal conformance (+6.2 pp vs one-shot B1, +2.4 pp vs test-feedback B2). E6 isolates **+7.7 pp** from full semantic IR over test-only feedback. Statistical tests show **M vs B2 conformance is not significant after Holm** (p=0.811); strict-success comparisons are uniformly non-significant (Holm p=1.000). Extended experiments (E10 random, E11 external SOFL, E12 multi-seed, E8b/c generalisation, B3–B5 self-refinement baselines) partially bound external validity but often cut against universal superiority claims.

## 3. Strengths

1. **Crisp contribution decomposition (Ch.1 §1.4, Fig. contribution-map).** Three Tier-1 insights (C1–C3) are separated from supporting engineering. Reviewers can evaluate claims without wading through a feature laundry list.

2. **Strong formal core (Ch.3).** SpecIR, first-match oracle, SMT witness suite, bounded acceptance, PDR/FAR, and the random-vs-SMT corollary are precise. The `classify_signal` running example (Ch.1 §1.3, Ch.3–4) is pedagogically effective and ties theory to mechanism.

3. **Mechanism-first evidence for C2 (Ch.7 §7.2, E6).** Feedback-variant isolation (+7.7 pp A→C with A≈B) is the paper’s best causal argument: specification-level structure in repair prompts matters beyond longer test dumps. This is more convincing than aggregate E1 tables.

4. **Honest statistical reporting (Ch.7 §7.8, `tables/stats_summary.tex`, Ch.6 §6.9).** The authors disclose non-significant M–B2 gaps, corrected win rate (6/120, not 73/120), post-hoc power (~2.5% for M vs B2), and scope claims to overlap-intensive specs. Rare and commendable.

5. **Overlap stratification and sampling analysis (Ch.7 §7.1/E3, §7.5/E4, E10 tertile figure).** E3/E4 show *where* SMT witnesses help (dense overlap, boundary coverage). This supports C1 mechanistically even when headline means are flat.

6. **Reproducibility posture (App. A, Ch.6 §6.2).** Versioned run IDs, JSONL logs, schema-validated IRs, scripted aggregation/plotting. Well above average for empirical SE papers.

7. **Failure analysis and deployment guidance (Ch.8 §8.1–8.4, E9).** Residual taxonomy (ordering 31%, boundary 27%, arithmetic 18%) and the B2-vs-M deployment boundary table show the authors understand limits, not just wins.

## 4. Weaknesses

1. **Author-built synthetic benchmark with built-in method advantage (Ch.6 §6.3, App. B, Ch.3 §8.6).** The 120-task corpus is generated by `HardTaskGenerator` and filtered for Z3 satisfiability plus **overlap-heavy guards** (criterion C3: discriminative difficulty *requires* overlapping guards). This is exactly the regime where SMT witnesses dominate random tests (E4, Cor. 3.1). Industrial SOFL prevalence of such tasks is unestablished. **Actionable:** Lead with overlap-scoped claims in title/abstract; add a preregistered industrial held-out set or community benchmark; report effect sizes *only* within overlap tertiles as primary.

2. **Primary comparative claim vs B2 is statistically null (Ch.7 Table 7.1, `stats_summary.tex`).** M vs B2 conformance: Holm **p=0.811**, Cliff’s δ=+0.0002 (negligible); M wins **6/120** paired strict-conformance tasks (~5%). M vs B1 is only marginal (Holm p=0.075, small δ=+0.036). A skeptical PC will treat +2.4 pp over B2 as noise on n=120 with heavy multiple testing. **Actionable:** Reframe RQ1 around E6/E3/E2 PDR–FAR; demote aggregate M–B2 conformance from abstract bullets; power analysis should drive sample-size planning before submission.

3. **Single LLM family, single-repeat primary protocol (Ch.6 §6.2–6.3, Ch.7).** All modes use **Qwen2.5-27B-Instruct** only. E1 is **repeat~0** (840 core runs + extended B3–B5). Bootstrap CIs over tasks do not substitute for run-level LLM replication. E12 (30 tasks × 3 seeds) shows **B2 > M** (89.2% vs 86.3%), **0/30 wins for M**, high M variance (σ=16.4), Friedman p=0.221, ranking unstable. **Actionable:** At minimum 3 seeds on full 120 tasks for B1/B2/M; add one proprietary and one open-weight model to test pipeline-not-model claim.

4. **Supplementary benchmarks often contradict headline narrative (Ch.7 §7.6–7.8).** E10 (uniform random, n≈100): **B2 88.0% > M 80.5%**, M wins 1/100. E11 (external curated SOFL, n=22): **B1/B2 tie 89.8% > M 81.9%**. E8c HumanEval-FSF: **B2 98.8% > M 87.1%**. These are reported honestly but undermine “HSP-Agile beats test-feedback” as a general claim. **Actionable:** Make deployment-boundary table (Ch.8) the primary contribution framing; treat E1 as stress-test, not representative sample.

5. **Full pipeline M is not Pareto-optimal on conformance (Ch.7 Table 7.1).** **A2 (no pattern guard) achieves 90.9% Conf. vs M’s 90.4%** at ~3.6× lower latency (12.1 s vs 43.7 s). Pattern guard adds prevention value (PDR/FAR) but not mean conformance. **Actionable:** Quantify escaped-defect cost in user study or industrial pilot; justify hard RF07 blocking vs advisory mode with FAR deltas per pattern.

6. **Strict acceptance rate undermines “prevention” framing (Ch.7, Ch.8 §8.3).** M **Accept** rate is **25.0%** vs B1/B2 **26.7%** (Holm p=1.000). **75%** of tasks remain best-effort after K=3 (E9). PDR 95% / FAR 5% are on **mutants**, not production tasks. **Actionable:** Report conformance-at-release vs best-effort separately; clarify what “prevented” means for the 75% non-accepted outputs in a sprint workflow.

7. **Thesis scale vs conference fit (entire manuscript).** Nine chapters + three appendices far exceed a 10–12 page SANER/ICSME paper. Method + implementation + formalization dominate; the empirical punch is diluted. B3–B5 in the main table (planned removal) is the right trim—also consider moving Ch.5 implementation detail to appendix. **Actionable:** Conference cut should foreground E6 + E3 + PDR/FAR + deployment boundary; demote formalization to 1–1.5 pages.

8. **VerifierLoop-FSF baseline (Ch.8 §8.5, E13).** **Addressed in revision:** mode B6 implements SMT-in-loop repair with `verifier_loop` feedback (no Semantic Feedback IR, no pattern guard). On 30-task stratified subset, B6≈B2 (86.0% vs 86.2%); on 100-task export B6 reaches 78.6% vs M 90.4% on E1—IR fields still matter beyond raw witnesses.

## 6. Questions for Authors

1. **M vs B2:** With Holm p=0.811 and δ≈0 on n=120, why should a practitioner pay **4.6× latency** for +2.4 pp mean conformance? What is the minimum detectable effect size your benchmark was powered for (your power analysis suggests ~180M tasks for 80% power at observed δ)?

2. **Benchmark circularity:** Criterion C3 *requires* overlapping guards—the same structure SMT witnesses target. On E10 without the overlap filter, B2 beats M (88.0% vs 80.5%). Is the honest claim “SMT helps on overlap-heavy FSF” rather than “specification-guided prevention beats test-feedback”?

3. **E12 instability:** Multi-seed runs show B2 leading M (89.2% vs 86.3%), 0/30 wins, σ=16.4 for M, Friedman p=0.221. Why should we trust single-repeat E1 rankings when a 30-task stratified subset already reverses them?

4. **A2 vs M:** A2 beats M on conformance (90.9% vs 90.4%) at 3.6× lower latency. For a sprint team, what quantitative FAR/escaped-defect cost justifies mandatory pattern-guard blocking over A2 + advisory RF warnings?

5. **E11 external corpus:** On 22 curated non-synthetic SOFL tasks, M trails B1/B2 (81.9% vs 89.8%). Does this falsify external validity, or confirm that M should only deploy on dense-overlap specs? How many industrial tasks did you measure overlap density on?

6. **B3–B5 fairness:** Self-Refine/Self-Debug/Reflexion (B3–B5) lack formal witnesses yet B4 reaches 87.1% (near B2’s 88.0%). Does E6’s +7.7 pp survive when B4 receives equal token budget and execution-trace feedback length-matched to Semantic Feedback IR?

7. **Accept vs prevention:** Only 25% of tasks reach Accept; 75% exit as best-effort with ordering/boundary residues (E9). What should a CI gate do with best-effort outputs—block merge, warn, or ship?

8. **Model generalization:** All results use Qwen-27B. Have you run even a 30-task pilot with GPT-4o or Claude to test whether the M–B2 gap widens on stronger models that may need less repair?

## 8. Comparison to Related Work — Is Positioning Convincing?

**Partially convincing, not fully.**

**What works:** The paper correctly situates itself between (a) contest-style LLM codegen (Codex/HumanEval mindset), (b) annotation-heavy DafnyBench deductive verification, and (c) Clover-style verifier loops that do not encode **first-match guard precedence** or measure PDR/FAR. Table 2.1 (Ch.2) and the DafnyBench/Clover discussion (Ch.8 §8.5) are directionally right. Positioning SgDP as a **bounded, annotation-free prevention layer** for Agile-SOFL sprint artefacts is credible and differentiated from repository coding agents (OpenHands, SWE-agent).

**What does not convince:**

- The uniqueness claim—“no prior approach combines FSF first-match semantics, LLM synthesis, CEGIS repair with SMT counterexamples, and pattern guard under PDR/FAR” (Ch.2)—is a **feature checklist**, not evidence of empirical margin. Clover + SMT + structured feedback is conceptually adjacent; without VerifierLoop-FSF the gap is asserted, not measured.
- **B3–B5** now bracket self-refinement, but B4 (87.1%) is close to B2 (88.0%), suggesting execution-trace feedback captures much of the repair benefit without SpecIR or Semantic Feedback IR.
- **DafnyBench** comparison is fair on the cost spectrum but the paper does not quantify how often finite witnesses accept incorrect implementations that proofs would reject (Theorem FAR bound is conservative, not empirical on full domain).
- **RF01–RF14 pattern catalogue** is largely ecosystem carryover from Li et al. IET fault-prevention work; the novel piece is LLM-loop integration, not pattern discovery.
- **E8b adapter corpora** show M > B1 but absolute conformance 23–42%—framework transfer is demonstrated, but practical utility on Mini-Z/Mini-StateMachine is weak.

**Verdict:** Positioning is **scholarly and increasingly self-aware** after revision (honest E10/E11/E12, power analysis, deployment boundary). For acceptance at SANER/ICSME, a skeptical PC will still want **(i)** non-synthetic primary evaluation or industrial replication, **(ii)** significant or consistently directional win over B2 under multi-seed/multi-model protocols, and **(iii)** a fair VerifierLoop-FSF or Clover-adapted FSF baseline. Until then, the paper is best classified as a **strong mechanism study** with **inconclusive comparative efficacy**.
