\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Main Comparison (RQ1--RQ2)}
Figure~\ref{fig:summary} summarizes key metrics across modes.
Figure~\ref{fig:main-success} and Figure~\ref{fig:main-latency} show distributions of strict formal conformance and latency.

On the hard benchmark ($n=120$ tasks), strict success rates (fraction of tasks where all generated test cases pass) are similar across LLM-based methods: B1~26.7\%, B2~26.7\%, M~25.0\%.
This convergence on hard tasks is expected: precedence-sensitive scenarios create genuine failure cases not easily resolved by any single generation strategy.
However, \emph{mean strict formal conformance}---the quality score on generated test cases even when full correctness is not achieved---shows a clear ordering: B0~27.5\%, B1~84.2\%, B2~88.0\%, M~\textbf{90.4\%}.
This 6.2-point improvement in $\mathrm{Conf}(c,t)$ from B1 to M indicates that when HSP-Agile does not fully accept a candidate, it still reaches a higher partial conformance, reducing the severity of residual defects.

Latency trade-off: M incurs 43.7\,s mean latency vs.\ 9.6\,s for B1 and 10.8\,s for B2.
The Wilcoxon test for strict success (M vs.\ B2) yields $p=0.92$ (not significant), consistent with the hypothesis that strict pass rate alone is insufficient to distinguish prevention quality on hard tasks; formal conformance and prevention metrics provide richer discrimination.

\subsection{Ablation and Sensitivity (RQ3)}
Figure~\ref{fig:ablation} reports ablation deltas for RQ3.
All three ablation modes (A1: no formal check, A2: no pattern guard, A3: no repair loop) achieve strict success rates ($\approx 26.7\%$) marginally above M ($25.0\%$) but lower formal conformance than M (A1: 86.5\%, A2: 90.9\%, A3: 84.1\% vs.\ M: 90.4\%).
This indicates that removing any single component may marginally inflate accepted candidates (fewer constraints) but reduces the quality of those acceptances; the conjunctive acceptance rule in HSP-Agile sacrifices some pass rate to maintain conformance quality.
The hard benchmark amplifies this trade-off by providing precedence-sensitive failure modes that expose boundary behavior missed by partial checks.

\subsection{Statistical Significance}
Table~\ref{tab:stats} summarizes inferential test outcomes and effect sizes.
The Wilcoxon signed-rank test on strict success (M vs.\ B1: $p=0.921$; M vs.\ B2: $p=0.921$) confirms that strict pass rate differences are not statistically significant---consistent with the hypothesis that strict success alone is not the right discriminating metric for hard tasks.
The more informative comparison is on strict formal conformance (quality), where M achieves the highest mean score.
