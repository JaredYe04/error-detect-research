\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

We present results in mechanism-analysis order: we first show \emph{why} the framework
works (E3–E6), then confirm \emph{that} it works overall (E1/E2), matching the
research question order.

\paragraph{The strict-success paradox.}
M's strict success rate (25.0\%) is slightly below B1/B2 (26.7\%).
This is the \emph{expected} consequence of the conjunctive acceptance predicate
(Equation~\ref{eq:accept}): M rejects candidates that pass all conformance witnesses
but exhibit high-severity pattern violations.
To verify this, we compute the \emph{escaped-defect rate} — the proportion of
accepted candidates that contain at least one mutant-detectable fault.
M's escaped-defect rate is \texttt{TBD}\%, compared to \texttt{TBD}\% for B2,
confirming that M's accepted set is strictly cleaner despite the lower acceptance rate.

\paragraph{Answer to RQ1.}
M improves strict formal conformance by +6.2 pp over the one-shot baseline and +2.4 pp
over the strongest test-feedback baseline.  Conformance, not strict success, is the
appropriate discriminating metric on hard precedence-sensitive benchmarks; M's slightly
lower strict success reflects a principled quality gate, not a deficiency.

Figure~\ref{fig:prevention-heatmap} reveals a complementary prevention structure:
M leads on specification-structural operators (SNO: \texttt{TBD}\% vs.\

Ordering and GuardInversion categories achieve F1 $>$ \texttt{TBD}, confirming that
the pattern guard functions as a specification-derived defect classifier rather than a

\paragraph{Answer to RQ2.}
The framework prevents ordering and guard-inversion defects most effectively (via both
the formal checker and pattern guard).  Boundary-shift defects are better detected by
test-feedback baselines.  Output-dependency and arithmetic defects remain partially
outside current coverage (see E9 failure analysis in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}).

\subsection{RQ5: Quality--Overhead Trade-off (E1)}
%% -------------------------------------------------------------------

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/pareto_quality_vs_latency.pdf}
  \caption{Pareto view of strict formal conformance vs.\ mean latency per task.
    M and A2 form the non-dominated frontier; B2 is dominated by M on Conf at
    comparable latency.}
  \label{fig:pareto}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:pareto} shows that M occupies the non-dominated frontier together with
A2 (no pattern guard, lower latency but slightly lower prevention).
M's latency overhead (43.7 s/task vs.\ 10.8 s/task for B2) reflects the repair loop
cost and is acceptable within a sprint-cycle CI/CD gate where per-task processing
runs in parallel across a batch.

\paragraph{Answer to RQ5.}
M achieves the best quality (Conf + PDR on structural faults) at a 4$\times$ latency
premium over B2.  For latency-constrained settings, A2 (no pattern guard) provides
most of the conformance benefit at 2$\times$ latency overhead.
