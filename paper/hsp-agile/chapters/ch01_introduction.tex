\chapter{Introduction}
\label{ch:intro}

Formal specification languages give software requirements a mathematical precision that
informal descriptions cannot achieve.  Yet this precision creates a new risk when
implementations are generated by large language models (LLMs): the model has no mechanism
to represent or respect the specification's semantic contract, and produces code that is
statistically plausible but formally incorrect.  We call the resulting failure class
\emph{specification-conformance defects}---faults where an LLM-generated implementation
satisfies most inputs yet violates the formal contract at the boundary regions that
formal specifications are specifically designed to characterise.  These defects are
systematically missed by existing test-based repair loops, which sample the input space
without knowledge of specification-level boundary structure.

This report asks: \emph{is there a general framework that can prevent
specification-conformance defects in LLM-generated code, using the specification itself
as the primary evidence source, without requiring full deductive verification?}
We answer affirmatively and present the \textbf{Specification-guided Defect Prevention
Framework} (\textsc{SgDP}), with \textbf{HSP-Agile} as its primary instantiation over
ordered guard-based formal specifications (SOFL/FSF in our benchmark).


\section{Context and Motivation}
\label{sec:intro:motivation}

The Structured Object-oriented Formal Language (SOFL) \citep{liu1997sofl,liu2014sofl}
provides the Functional Specification Format (FSF): ordered scenarios pairing guard predicates
with postconditions under first-match precedence semantics.  Agile-SOFL \citep{li2022agilesofl}
embeds FSF tasks in sprint-based development as design documents, oracles, and acceptance
criteria.  LLM-assisted generation \citep{chen2021codex,openai2023gpt4} accelerates this
workflow but synthesises by pattern completion rather than guard-order semantics
\citep{dakhel2023copilot,lynn2024verifierloop}.  Overlapping guard regions expose
precedence-sensitive boundary faults that standard test suites miss
\citep{zhu1997specification}.


\section{Problem Statement}
\label{sec:intro:problem}

The central problem is a bounded acceptance decision over the formal
specification lifecycle.
Given task~$t$ with ordered scenarios $\mathcal{S}_t = (S_1,\ldots,S_n)$ and synthesis
budget~$K$, determine whether any candidate~$c$ satisfies
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{Accept}(c, t)
  \;\iff\;
  \mathrm{Conf}(c, t) \geq \tau
  \;\wedge\;
  P^H(c) = 0,
  \label{eq:acceptance}
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{Conf}(c,t)\in[0,1]$ is the scenario-level conformance score,
$\tau=1.0$ in this work, and $P^H(c)=0$ asserts absence of high-severity fault patterns
under static screening.  Witness construction for scenario~$S_i$ requires inputs satisfying
$G_i(\mathbf{x}) \wedge \bigwedge_{j<i}\neg G_j(\mathbf{x})$---a conjunction that
standard sampling heuristics cannot reliably reach \citep{barrett2018smt}.

Consider the \code{classify\_signal} specification from our benchmark:

\section{Research Challenges}
\label{sec:intro:challenges}

\subsection{Closing the Specification--Code Semantic Gap}
Verifying conformance to scenario~$S_i$ requires inputs in
$G_i \wedge \bigwedge_{j<i}\neg G_j$, often a low-measure region missed by random sampling.
SMT solvers \citep{demoura2008z3,barrett2018smt} enable directed witness generation but
require a faithful, sound FSF encoding.

\subsection{Structured Counterexample-Guided Repair}
Test-feedback loops lack specification-level structure \citep{lynn2024verifierloop}; the LLM
may patch a surfaced counterexample without fixing guard precedence.  Effective repair demands
prompts identifying the violated scenario, the ordering constraint, and the SMT witness.

\subsection{Operationalising Prevention within a Bounded Budget}
Full verification is impractical for unannotated LLM output
\citep{leino2010dafny,loughman2024dafnybench}.  The challenge is calibrated prevention:
stronger than test-feedback \citep{chen2021codex}, weaker than deductive proof, and fast
enough for sprint budgets \citep{beck2001xp,li2022agilesofl}.


\section{Proposed Approach}
\label{sec:intro:approach}

\textsc{SgDP} addresses these challenges through four specification-grounded components:
a \emph{Specification Oracle} derived from the formal spec; a \emph{Constraint-guided
Witness Generator} that encodes guard precedence as SMT constraints; a \emph{Scenario-aware
Semantic Feedback IR} structuring counterexamples at the specification level; and a
conjunctive \emph{Acceptance Predicate} gating release on formal conformance and
specification-derived quality criteria.  \textbf{HSP-Agile} instantiates this architecture
over SOFL/FSF in a four-stage pipeline (synthesis, formal checking, counterexample-guided
repair, pattern guard).  Chapter~\ref{ch:method} presents the framework formally, including
three correctness theorems; Chapter~\ref{ch:results} demonstrates additional notation
instantiations beyond SOFL/FSF via the SpecIR adapter layer.

\section{Contributions}
\label{sec:intro:contributions}

This report makes five concrete contributions (Figure~\ref{fig:contribution-map}).

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \tikzinput{diagrams/tikz/contribution_map}
  \caption{Overview of \textsc{SgDP} research contributions.  The five contributions form an integrated framework from pipeline design through empirical validation.}
  \label{fig:contribution-map}
\end{figure}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item[\textbf{C1.}] \textbf{Specification-guided Defect Prevention Framework (\textsc{SgDP}).}
    Canonical SpecIR layer with pluggable adapters (SOFL/FSF, Mini-Z, Mini-SM) and
    HSP-Agile FSF lowerer; four-component prevention architecture.
  \item[\textbf{C2.}] \textbf{Strict Acceptance Predicate and Semantic Feedback IR.}
    Definition of $\mathrm{Accept}(c,t)$; nine-field JSON IR
    (\texttt{schemas/semantic\_feedback.schema.json}) with renderer-separated repair prompts.
  \item[\textbf{C3.}] \textbf{Precedence-Sensitive Hard Benchmark.}
    120 synthetic tasks with overlapping guards, plus real-derived and multi-notation subsets.
  \item[\textbf{C4.}] \textbf{Two-Layer Prevention Evaluation Protocol.}
    PDR/FAR measurement via specification-confusion and implementation-screening mutants.
  \item[\textbf{C5.}] \textbf{Empirical Evidence and Reproducible Artifacts.}
    90.4\% mean strict conformance (+6.2~pp over one-shot LLM); full logs and scripts released.
\end{enumerate}
