\chapter{Experimental Results}
\label{ch:results}

This chapter tests one hypothesis:
\emph{structured semantic feedback plus a conjunctive release gate reduces
escaped defects on thin specification regions that mean pass rate hides.}
The evidence ladder is fixed (Table~\ref{tab:evidence-ladder}):
mechanism under repair headroom (E6), prevention under the conjunctive gate
(E2), equal-$K$ Conf.\ ranking, deployment guidance (C4), and one public ACL
interception case.
Near-ceiling ranking corpora (E1/E10/E12) and historical failure archives (E9)
appear only as saturation context in Section~\ref{sec:results:rq5} and
Appendix~\ref{app:stress}.

After the first-match others-witness measurement correction, fixed-oracle
aggregate Conf.\ sits near the ceiling (M and B2 both ${\ge}98\%$ on the hard
set).
Those figures are comparable Conf., not the safety claim; the claim is
$\mathrm{Accept}\Leftrightarrow\mathrm{Conf}{=}1\wedge\mathrm{Screen}{=}\mathit{pass}$.

\section{RQ1: Typed Semantic Feedback IR}
\label{sec:results:rq1}
%=============================================================================

Does naming the failed specification region improve repair beyond unstructured
test dumps?
The primary answer is E6 under full mode~M at matched $K{=}3$.
Supporting runs (E5 budget shape, E14 structured-family scope, hard combo
seeds, fixed-oracle component ablation, E17 advisory gate) refine that answer;
they are not co-equal headlines.

\subsection{Catch-All Rescue under Typed Feedback (E6)}
\label{sec:results:e6}

Return to the tier classifier of Chapter~\ref{ch:intro}: Reject and Pass bands
are busy; the mid-band \texttt{OTHERWISE} must return \texttt{Review}; an LLM
candidate collapses it to \texttt{Pass}.
Busy unit tests stay green.
When a rare residual witness finally fails, an unstructured dump says only
that ``a test failed.''
Typed Semantic Feedback IR names the residual \texttt{others} scenario, the
guard, and the output mismatch---and that naming is what E6 isolates.
(The intro vignette uses three scenarios with residual $S_3$; BENCH-120 hard
tasks scale the same first-match skeleton to eight scenarios, so the residual
is scenario index~8 / $S_8$ in the coded wins below.)

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering

  \caption{\textbf{Claim:} specification-level structure, not prompt volume, drives
    repair.
    \textbf{How to read:} A~=~test only (no scenario info); B~=~test+expected;
    C~=~full Semantic Feedback IR.  Compare bar heights, not bar widths.}
  \label{fig:feedback-variant}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:feedback-variant} holds mode~M fixed and varies only feedback
content ($n{=}120$ tasks per variant; Table~\ref{tab:feedback-e6};
\texttt{run\_feedback\_v2}).
Variant~C (full Semantic Feedback IR) reaches mean Conf\,=\,86.9\%---a gain of
+7.7\,pp over test-only feedback (A, 79.1\%) and +8.0\,pp over
test-plus-expected (B, 78.9\%).
Paired per-task statistics (Table~\ref{tab:e6-paired}): C vs.\ A
wins/losses/ties 14/4/102; bootstrap 95\% CI on mean $\Delta$Conf.\
$[2.3,\,13.6]$\,pp; Wilcoxon signed-rank $p{=}0.018$ (nonzero deltas).
C vs.\ B: same W/L/T, ${+}8.0$\,pp, CI $[2.2,\,14.0]$, $p{=}0.027$.
Both intervals exclude zero.
A and B are statistically indistinguishable on aggregate means
($\Delta < 0.3$\,pp): adding the expected string alone is not a substitute for
naming the violated scenario.

\subsection{Repair Budget Shape (E5)}

If most of the conformance gain arrives by the second attempt, budget $K{=}3$
is enough; further calls buy little on this benchmark.
Figure~\ref{fig:convergence} settles that question for mode~M.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \widefig{figures/repair_convergence.pdf}
  \caption{\textbf{Claim:} most repair gain arrives by iteration~2, justifying
    budget $K=3$.
    \textbf{How to read:} solid line is mode~M; compare to B2 at the same
    attempt index.  Shaded bands are 95\% bootstrap CIs.}
  \label{fig:convergence}
\end{figure}

The $\mathrm{Conf}(k)$ trajectory, extracted from 258 backfilled M/B2
\texttt{attempt\_history} records, rises from 78.3\% at $k{=}1$ to 84.5\% at
$k{=}2$, then plateaus at 83.4\% at $k{=}3$.
Most of the repair gain arrives within two iterations; E6's matched $K{=}3$
protocol therefore sits at a natural budget sweet spot rather than an
arbitrary truncation.

\subsection{Structured-Surface Comparison (E14)}
\label{sec:results:e14}

E6 contrasts typed IR with \emph{unstructured} dumps.
E14 asks a different question: within length-matched \emph{structured}
feedback, is bare Semantic Feedback IR uniquely best?
Table~\ref{tab:e14-feedback} holds task count fixed ($n{=}120$) and sweeps
four variants under mode~M (\texttt{run\_e14\_sweep\_v1}).
\texttt{execution\_trace\_matched} (85.4\% mean strict conformance) exceeds
\texttt{semantic\_ir} (75.1\%) and test-only surfaces (73.8--74.4\%).
Paired per-task mean Conf.\ (\texttt{e14\_paired\_summary}):
\texttt{semantic\_ir} vs.\ \texttt{execution\_trace\_matched} wins 5 / losses
19 / ties 96 (${-}10.3$\,pp); vs.\ \texttt{test\_only} wins 18 / losses 16 /
ties 86 (${+}1.3$\,pp).
E14 therefore compares length-matched structured surfaces; it does not
replace E6 as the primary single-factor lever against unstructured feedback.

\paragraph{Design rule.}
Unstructured test dumps are the wrong baseline when SpecIR witnesses expose
scenario-local failures: naming the failed region (E6) moves mean Conf.\ under
repair headroom---often as a rescue when test-only Conf.\ collapses
(Table~\ref{tab:e6-win-profile}).
Within the structured family, length-matched execution traces can beat bare
Semantic Feedback IR on aggregate Conf.\ (E14), while scenario-indexed fields
remain the right artefact when engineers need localisation, auditable repair
prompts, or cross-notation rendering from SpecIR.
Default strengthened mode~M may therefore render
\texttt{execution\_trace\_matched} for Conf.; controlled E6 still isolates
\texttt{semantic\_ir} vs.\ \texttt{test\_only} as the causal C2 lever.

\section{RQ3: Conjunctive Accept vs.\ Conf Alone}
\label{sec:results:rq3}
%=============================================================================

Catch-all rescue explains how typed feedback repairs residual failures under
headroom.
Prevention asks a complementary question: given a faulty candidate, does the
conjunctive gate refuse release when unit-test feedback would still accept?
Ordering and guard-inversion mutants are the secondary vignette here---the
fault classes E6's Conf.\ delta does not measure on BENCH-120, but that the
gate must still catch.

\paragraph{Vignette (mutant rejection).}
Take a correct \code{classify\_signal} implementation and apply an SNO mutant
that swaps $S_1$ and $S_2$ in the oracle.
Mode~M's witness $(-3,-10)$ still expects \texttt{Error} under the mutated
order only if the candidate follows the new text; a candidate that kept the
original branch order fails $\mathrm{Accept}$ and counts toward PDR.
A WRO mutant that merely reorders \texttt{if} branches in the candidate is
likewise rejected when the witness set still encodes first-match priority.

What Figure~\ref{fig:prevention-heatmap} adds is complementarity, not a single
winner.
M leads on specification-structural operators (scenario reordering, guard
inversion); B2 leads on boundary-shift operators.
SMT witnesses sit on first-match boundaries and expose ordering faults, while
B2's tests sometimes hit boundary inputs without naming the violated scenario.

Table~\ref{tab:prevention} summarises the implementation-screening track only
($n{=}852$ mutants per mode), not the pooled E2 corpus ($n{=}1{,}704$ when
spec-confusion and implementation-screening are combined; see
\texttt{prevention\_summary.json}).
B1 lacks an explicit rejection mechanism (FAR 100\%), while M improves both
PDR (+3.8\,pp over B2) and FAR ($-$3.8\,pp) on this fixed mutant set.
A paired bootstrap over mutant IDs ($B{=}5{,}000$, seed~42;
\texttt{e2\_pdr\_far\_bootstrap.json}) yields
$\Delta\mathrm{PDR}(\mathrm{M}{-}\mathrm{B2})$ 95\% CI
$[2.5,\,5.0]$\,pp and $\Delta\mathrm{FAR}$ 95\% CI
$[-5.0,\,{-}2.5]$\,pp---both intervals exclude zero.
Per-operator complementarity is visible in
Figure~\ref{fig:prevention-heatmap}, not in the aggregate alone.
Per-category F1 in Figure~\ref{fig:pattern-f1} reinforces the structural
prevention story: Ordering and GuardInversion achieve the highest recall.

\paragraph{Answer to RQ3 / C3.}
Ordering and guard-inversion defects are prevented most effectively;
boundary shifts favour B2; arithmetic/output residues remain
(Chapter~\ref{ch:discussion}).
Equal-$K$ Conf.\ ranking (\texttt{M\_eq}, Section~\ref{sec:results:rq4})
shows M can match or exceed B2 on mean Conf.\ at $K{=}3$, but prevention
value of the conjunctive gate is read from E2 PDR/FAR under the shared mutant
protocol---not from Conf.\ alone.

\section{RQ4: Deployment Boundary (M vs.\ B2)}
\label{sec:results:rq4}
%=============================================================================

Mechanism (E6) and prevention (E2) motivate when to escalate beyond test
feedback.
Fair Conf.\ ranking at matched budget, external pilots, and one public ACL
case complete the deployment rule: prefer B2 by default; escalate to full~M
when conjunctive $\mathrm{Accept}$, prevention (PDR/FAR), or typed Semantic
Feedback IR is required---and repair headroom remains.

\subsection{Equal-$K$ Conf.\ Ranking}

Fixed-oracle E1 M-win compares strengthened~M ($K{=}5$ bundle) to B2
($K{=}3$); that ranking is saturation context
(Section~\ref{sec:results:rq5}), not the fair Conf.\ comparison.
The matched-budget ranking is \texttt{run\_e1\_equal\_k\_v1}:
\texttt{M\_eq} ($K{=}3$, \texttt{semantic\_ir}, advisory gate) reaches
100.0\% vs.\ B2 97.5\% (${+}2.5$\,pp; paired 3/0/117).
This is the matched-budget Conf.\ ranking for C4, not a substitute for E6's
within-M feedback isolation (+7.7\,pp).

\subsection{Deployment Synthesis}

Table~\ref{tab:deployment-boundary} synthesises the deployment rule from
E3 (non-monotone tertiles on canonical E1; Section~\ref{sec:results:rq2}),
E10 (unfiltered: M ${+}1.0$\,pp near ceiling), E12 (B2 slightly more
seed-stable), E11 (external SOFL), and E8c (real-derived slices).
E6 remains the mechanism anchor (+7.7\,pp typed IR under controlled
comparison); E2 supplies the prevention rationale for deploying the
conjunctive gate.
Supporting sampling evidence appears in Section~\ref{sec:results:e4}
(SMT vs.\ random boundary coverage).

\paragraph{Answer to RQ4 / C4.}
Prefer B2 by default; escalate to~M when release requires
$\mathrm{Accept}{=}1$ or target FAR${\le}5\%$ \emph{and} repair headroom
remains (E6 rescue when test-only Conf.\ collapses).
Do not escalate from overlap tertiles alone (E3 non-monotone).
External pilots often tie B2 with \texttt{M\_eq} and reinforce the default
(Table~\ref{tab:deployment-boundary}).
Versus VerifierLoop-FSF (B6): full~M's irreducible increment is conjunctive
$\mathrm{Accept}$ plus PDR/FAR (and E6 typed IR when operating inside M)---not
aggregate Conf.\ dominance over B6 or B2 on near-ceiling corpora.
When contracts are simple enough that baselines already saturate, B2 is the
right choice; Section~\ref{sec:results:acl-case} shows when the gate still
earns its keep.

catch a realistic lucky-pass candidate?
Listing~\ref{lst:acl-fsf} is an ordered API ACL reconstructed from the
public RealSpec / harvest family (\texttt{enforce\_api\_acl}): deny guests;
require step-up MFA on high scope; permit narrow admin/reader bands; and
fail-closed on \texttt{OTHERWISE}.

Listing~\ref{lst:acl-bug} is the LLM-shaped failure that matters for release.
Busy unit tests---guest deny, admin permit---pass.
Two defects remain: (i)~high-scope without MFA incorrectly permits instead of
stepping up; (ii)~the catch-all residual fail-\emph{opens}.
Mean pass rate on a domestic-style suite can look excellent.
An SMT witness for the residual
$(\mathit{role}{=}2,\mathit{scope}{=}2,\mathit{mfa}{=}1)$ forces
\texttt{OTHERWISE}; the candidate returns $\mathit{permit}{=}1$.
Conjunctive Accept therefore yields $\mathrm{Conf}<1$ and refuses release
(Listing~\ref{lst:acl-ir}).
A pluggable high-severity screen can additionally flag fail-open residuals
even when a sparse suite happens to miss them.

This single public case is not a vendor production trace.
It is the external-validity object the evaluation needs: a reproducible
ordered-guard contract where tests look green and Accept stays red---the
decision-framework claim in operational form.
